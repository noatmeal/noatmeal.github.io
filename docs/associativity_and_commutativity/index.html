Distributivity, Associativity, and Commutativity

https://noatmeal.github.io/

In [1], we created some fundamental tools for working with the natural numbers. In this post, we’ll establish some of the core ideas that make working with addition and multiplication (as well as many other types of algebraic operations outside of the purview of this post) such a pleasure.

1 Preliminaries

We start by recalling the theorems from [1] that restated the definitions of addition and multiplication in algebraic terms where n+ is the image of n∈ℕ under the successor function.

Theorem 1.1.

n+1=n+ where n+ is the image of n∈ℕ under the successor function.

Theorem 1.2.

n+(m+1)=(n+m)+1

Lemma 1.1.

n⋅1=n

Theorem 1.3.

n⋅(m+1)=n+(n⋅m)

1.1 Induction

It’s often easier to work with Theorem 1.4 from [1] by restating it in an algebraic form.

Theorem 1.4 (Principle of Mathematical Induction).

If S is an inductive subset of ℕ, then S=ℕ.

Theorem 1.5.

If S⊆ℕ, 1∈S, and for all n∈S it’s the case that n+1∈S, then S=ℕ.

Proof.

Considering the equality between n+1 and n+ as stated in Theorem 1.1, we can see that S is an inductive subset of ℕ and so the hypothesis of Theorem 1.4 is fulfilled and which leads us to conclude that S=ℕ. ∎

2 A Few “Nice to Have” Theorems

2.1 Addition

Theorem 2.1 (Associativity of Addition).

x+(y+z)=(x+y)+z for all x,y,z∈ℕ.

Proof.

We proceed by induction on z. Let S be the set of all natural numbers such that for n∈S we have x+(y+n)=(x+y)+n. Well by Theorem 1.2, we know that x+(y+1)=(x+y)+1 and so 1∈S. So suppose that n∈S. Using Theorem 1.2 and our inductive hypothesis that n∈S, we can see that

x+(y+(n+1)) =x+((y+n)+1)
=(x+(y+n))+1
=((x+y)+n)+1
=(x+y)+(n+1)

So by Theorem 1.5, S=ℕ. ∎

Lemma 2.1.

1+n=n+1

Proof.

We proceed by induction on n and let S be the set of all natural numbers such that 1+n=n+1. Since 1+1=1+1 we can see that 1∈S. Now let n∈S and consider 1+(n+1). Well by Theorem 1.2 and our inductive hypothesis, 1+(n+1)=(1+n)+1=(n+1)+1 and so we conclude that S=ℕ. ∎

Theorem 2.2 (Commutativity of Addition).

x+y=y+x for all x,y∈ℕ

Proof.

We proceed by induction on y and let S be the set of all natural numbers such that x+y=y+x. Our base case of 1∈S is established by Lemma 2.1 and so let’s assume that n∈S and consider x+(n+1). Well then using Theorem 1.2, Lemma 2.1, and our inductive hypothesis, we can see that

x+(n+1) =(x+n)+1
=(n+x)+1
=n+(x+1)
=n+(1+x)
=(n+1)+x

and so S=ℕ. ∎

2.2 Multiplication

Preliminary Remark:

We’ll be referring to x⋅y as just x⁢y as well as use Theorem 2.2 and Theorem 2.1 without reference throughout these proofs.

2.3 Distributivity

Lemma 2.2 (Left Distributivity of Multiplication).

x⁢(y+z)=x⁢y+z⁢x for all x,y,z∈ℕ.

Proof.

We proceed by induction on z. We use Theorem 1.3 to see that

x⁢(y+1) =x+x⁢y
=x⁢y+x
=x⁢y+x⋅1

Now suppose that x⁢(y+n)=x⁢y+x⁢n for n∈ℕ. We can see from using our inductive hypothesis and Theorem 1.3 that

x⁢(y+(n+1)) =x⁢((y+n)+1)
=x+x⁢(y+n)
=x+x⁢y+x⁢n
=x⁢y+x+x⁢n
=x⁢y+x⁢(n+1)

∎

Lemma 2.3 (Right Distributivity of Multiplication).

(y+z)⁢x=y⁢x+z⁢x for all x,y,z∈ℕ.

Proof.

We proceed by induction on x. First note that (y+z)⋅1=y+z=y⋅1+z⋅1. Now assume that (y+z)⁢n=y⁢n+z⁢n for some n∈ℕ. We can see from using our inductive hypothesis and Theorem 1.3 that

(y+z)⁢(n+1) =(y+z)+(y+z)⁢n
=y+z+y⁢n+z⁢n
=y+y⁢n+z+z⁢n
=y⁢(n+1)+z⁢(n+1)

∎

Theorem 2.3 (Distributivity of Multiplication).

x⁢(y+z)=x⁢y+x⁢z and (y+z)⁢x=y⁢x+z⁢x

Proof.

This is established by Lemma 2.3 and Lemma 2.2. ∎

2.4 Associativity and Commutativity

Theorem 2.4 (Associativity of Multiplication).
Lemma 2.4.

1⋅n=n⋅1

Proof.

We proceed by induction on n. Our base case is established by 1⋅1=1⋅1 and so suppose that 1⋅n=n⋅1 and consider 1⋅(n+1). Well by Theorem 1.3 we can see that 1⋅(n+1)=1+n⋅1=1+n=n+1=(n+1)⋅1. ∎

Theorem 2.5 (Commutativity of Multiplication).

x⁢y=y⁢x for all x,y∈ℕ
